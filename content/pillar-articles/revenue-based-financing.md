# Revenue-Based Financing for SaaS: The Complete Guide

## Introduction

Growth costs money. For SaaS companies, the traditional options — equity financing or venture debt — come with trade-offs that do not always align with your goals. Equity dilutes your ownership. Venture debt requires collateral and personal guarantees. Revenue-Based Financing (RBF) offers a third path: growth capital that flexes with your revenue and does not dilute your equity.

This guide explains how RBF works for SaaS, compares the top providers, and provides a financing calculator framework to help you choose the right capital strategy.

## What Is Revenue-Based Financing

RBF is a form of financing where a capital provider gives you an upfront advance in exchange for a fixed percentage of your ongoing revenue until a predetermined cap is reached. Unlike a loan with fixed payments, RBF payments scale with your revenue.

### How It Works in Practice

1. Application: You provide 12+ months of revenue data, bank connections, and basic company information
2. Underwriting: The provider analyzes your revenue growth, churn, margins, and unit economics
3. Offer: You receive a term sheet with the advance amount, revenue share percentage, cap, and term
4. Funding: Once accepted, funds typically arrive within 3-10 business days
5. Repayment: A fixed percentage of your daily or monthly revenue is automatically deducted until the cap is reached
6. Completion: Once you have repaid the cap, the obligation ends — no equity, no ongoing payments

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Advance Amount | The lump sum you receive upfront |
| Revenue Share % | The percentage of revenue paid to the provider |
| Cap | The maximum total amount you will repay (typically 1.2x-1.5x the advance) |
| Term | The expected repayment period (typically 12-36 months) |
| Minimum Payment | A floor payment regardless of revenue fluctuations |
| Prepayment | Option to pay off the balance early, often at a discount |

## When RBF Makes Sense vs Equity Financing vs Venture Debt

### RBF Is Best When

You have predictable recurring revenue (ideally $1M+ ARR). You want to avoid dilution and maintain control. You have strong gross margins (60%+). You need growth capital for specific initiatives. You want flexible payments that scale with revenue.

### Equity Financing Is Best When

You are pre-revenue or very early stage. You need large amounts of capital ($10M+ rounds). You want strategic investor connections and guidance. You are building toward a large exit outcome.

### Venture Debt Is Best When

You have already raised venture capital. You need to extend runway between equity rounds. You can meet fixed monthly payment obligations.

## How to Prepare Your Financials

Prepare 12+ months of revenue data, bank statements (3-6 months connected via Plaid), profit and loss statements, unit economics breakdown (CAC, LTV, payback period, gross margin), revenue concentration analysis, churn analysis, and 12-month growth projections.

Key metrics providers evaluate: Monthly MRR growth of 10%+ MoM, gross margin of 70%+, net revenue retention of 110%+, logo churn under 5% monthly, LTV/CAC ratio of 3:1 or higher, CAC payback under 12 months, and no single customer above 20% of revenue.

## Pros and Cons

### Pros

No dilution — you keep 100% ownership. No personal guarantees required. Payments flex with revenue. No board seats or investor control. Faster than equity fundraising (weeks vs months). Can be stacked with other financing. Predictable total cost with a fixed cap.

### Cons

More expensive than venture debt (effective APR of 15-35%). Daily or monthly revenue deductions impact cash flow. Requires predictable recurring revenue. Typically limited to companies with $500K+ ARR.

## Conclusion

Revenue-Based Financing has become an essential tool for SaaS founders who want to grow without giving up control. The best approach for most SaaS companies is a layered strategy: use RBF for growth capital between equity rounds, maintain a venture debt facility for runway extension, and raise equity when the valuation is right for your stage.
